OpenSWI: A Massive-Scale Benchmark Dataset for Surface Wave Dispersion Curve Inversion

Feng Liu, Sijie Zhao, Xinyu Gu, Fenghua Ling, Yaxing Li*, Rui Su*,
Peiqin Zhuang, Jianping Huang, Lei Bai
Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Shanghai Artificial Intelligence Laboratory
Nanjing University, Chengdu University of Technology
Paper Code arXiv

Workflow for generating 1-D velocity profiles from geological models for surface wave inversion.

Abstract

Surface wave dispersion curve inversion is essential for shallow resource exploration and deep geological studies, yet it remains challenged by low computational efficiency, sensitivity to initial models, and susceptibility to local minima. In recent years, data-driven deep learning methods, inspired by their success in computer vision and natural language processing, have shown great potential to enable more efficient and accurate inversion. Nevertheless, the lack of large-scale and diverse benchmark datasets for surface wave dispersion curves hinders the development and evaluation of deep learning algorithms. To fill this gap, we introduce OpenSWI, a large-scale benchmark dataset created by the proposed data construction process SWIDP. OpenSWI consists of three subsets tailored to different research scales and application scenarios: OpenSWI-shallow, OpenSWI-deep, and OpenSWI-real. The OpenSWI-shallow dataset is constructed from the 2-D geological model dataset OpenFWI and includes various shallow subsurface geological features (e.g., flat layers, faults, folds, and actual geological structure), comprising over 22 million 1-D velocity profiles paired with their corresponding dispersion curves. The OpenSWI-deep dataset is synthesized from 14 global and regional 3-D geological models, containing approximately 1.26 million high-fidelity 1-D dispersion data pairs. The OpenSWI-real dataset compiles two sets of real-world observed dispersion curves and their corresponding reference models, providing a solid foundation for evaluating generalization performance. To assess the utility of the datasets, we trained deep learning models on OpenSWI-shallow and OpenSWI-deep, and tested them on OpenSWI-real. The results show that models trained on OpenSWI yield inversion outputs highly consistent with real-world references.
